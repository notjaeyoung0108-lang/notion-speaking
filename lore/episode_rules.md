# Episode Rules

## Core Structure

1. One situation.
2. One problem.
3. One conversation topic.
4. Every panel directly responds to the previous panel.
5. Natural target sentence usage.
6. The character reaction chain is the spine of the episode.
7. Clear final character consequence; resolution is optional.
8. Each episode is a sitcom learning scene: a small warm conflict that teaches the
   speaking moment through character behavior and visible cause/effect.

---

## Conversation Rules

* The conversation must stay on the same topic from start to finish.
* No topic shifts.
* No unrelated observations.
* No introducing a second problem.

---

## Speaker Rules

* Character speakers alternate strictly.
* Object / setting / caption panels may appear between dialogue panels and do not count as a speaker.

Example:

A → object/caption → B → A

or

object/caption → A → B → object/caption → A

* Never allow the same character speaker twice in a row.

---

## Character Rules

* Characters support the conversation.
* Use signature traits only when naturally triggered.
* At most one major signature behavior per character in a scene.
* Comedy comes from character facet collision: two people bring different habits, fears,
  standards, filters, or coping styles to the same tiny problem.
* Do not use random jokes, wacky interruptions, memes, or punchlines that do not come from
  the characters' active facets.
* Clarity AND character together — never trade one for the other. Keep every line simple
  enough for a learner, AND make each character react in their own distinct way. Two people
  must never sound interchangeable; that difference in reaction is the heart of the scene.
* Voice lives in WHAT a character chooses to say, their attitude, and timing — not in
  difficult words. You can be deadpan, fussy, blunt, or over-eager using only plain English.

---

## Target Sentence Rules

The target sentence must emerge naturally.

The episode is not "a sitcom scene that happens to contain the sentence."
It is "these characters under pressure until this sentence becomes the natural thing to say."

Character causality is the main character. The target sentence is a required spoken move inside
that causality: someone says it because of what they want, protect, avoid, over-control, or
misread in this exact relationship moment.

For planning, define the speaking moment as a structure:

1. Situation — the ordinary state created by a character facet or relationship dynamic.
2. Pressure — the specific thing that activates a character's desire or defense.
3. Expression — the exact moment where the target sentence is the natural thing that character says.
4. Confirmation — the visible consequence of that character choice.

This character reaction chain is the episode spine. The target sentence must grow from it, not sit
beside it as a learning requirement.

Do not:

* explain it
* define it
* force it
* attach it as a side remark to an unrelated problem

Use the exact target sentence as written. Do not paraphrase or grammar-shift it.

---

## Character Beat Structure

Build the reader's understanding step by step:

1. Situation — show the everyday setup and the active character facet.
2. Pressure — create the specific reason this character's desire/defense gets triggered.
3. Expression — use the target sentence as that character's natural spoken action.
4. Confirmation — show the result, reaction, or small consequence of that character choice.

If the episode has more than 4 panels, use the extra panels to deepen the pressure or
aftermath without changing topic.

Every episode needs a visible learning moment: a clear before/after, object state, decision,
reaction, or consequence that lets the reader understand when someone would say the sentence
without a definition.

---

## Speaking Moment Carrier

Choose the clearest way to make the speaking moment visible:

* character-led — the sentence is clearest through two people reacting differently.
* object-led — the sentence is clearest through an object, document, screen, receipt, pile,
  label, timer, empty container, broken item, or visible before/after state.
* action-led — the sentence is clearest through someone doing a concrete action.
* setting-led — the sentence is clearest through the place or situation pressure itself.
* mixed — use characters, objects, and setting together.

Do not force character banter when a prop or visible state change would explain the phrase
faster. Characters may still appear, but their line choices must still come from their facets.

Use the smallest cast needed:

* 0 characters is allowed for object-led setup/proof panels only. The target sentence itself
  must still be spoken by a character.
* 1 character is allowed when one person's action is enough.
* 2 characters is the default for dialogue-led scenes.
* 3 characters only when the speaking moment truly needs a group.

---

## Visual Continuity

Keep location continuity, but do not force identical background tags every panel.

Use recurring locations from world.md as the default sets:

* Plaoud Office
* Meeting Room
* Rooftop
* Neighborhood Cafe
* Convenience Store Bench
* Hanyoil Apartment

The plan should name both the broad set and the specific room/area. The broad place should
stay coherent. Panel-specific details may change when they show the
character beat:

* scattered receipts → sorted receipt piles
* unread message → sent follow-up email
* empty container → replacement on the counter

Avoid random location jumps.

---

## English Rules

Use simple everyday English.

Each speech bubble should contain one simple idea.

Avoid:

* jargon
* advanced idioms
* niche references
* multiple ideas in one bubble

---

## Ending Rules

The final panel should feel like the next honest consequence of the character filters. It may be:

* the resolution of the problem
* the aftermath of the problem
* a dodge
* a relapse
* a correction
* a tiny escalation
* an awkward residue

Humor is optional. Include a punchline, deadpan reaction, or small ironic twist only when it
arises naturally from the situation and the character facet collision. The joke must be
immediately understandable and make the speaking moment clearer.

---

## Failure Cases

* topic jumping
* unrelated scenes
* repeated speakers
* forced target sentence
* confusing references
* introducing a second problem
* character bit hijacking the speaking moment
* random jokes that do not come from character facet collision
* sitcom conflict that does not make the target sentence feel necessary
* missing visible learning moment
* object/action/setting panels that do not clearly carry the speaking moment
